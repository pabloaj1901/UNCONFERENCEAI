PMO unCON 2026 · AI & Innovation Track · AI-1
Facilitator Script — Session AI-1
March 24, morning  ·  Exploring AI Opportunities for the PMO: From Ideas to Practical Use  ·  This is a guided provocation, not a lecture. The framing opens the room — the room provides the content.
1
Opening
The Provocation — Before Any Content
~0:00–0:05
Goal: break the assumption that everyone in the room means the same thing when they say "AI". Create a productive unsettling before content begins.

"Before we talk about AI opportunities for the PMO, let's test something simple: when people say 'AI', are we even talking about the same thing?"

Make a real pause here. Let the question sit. A few people will smile — they know where this is going. Others will look slightly unsure. That tension is the point.

"Most organizations are not failing at AI because they lack ideas. They fail because they mix together very different things under one label."

This opens the door to sorting the terrain — which is the entire job of AI-1 before any use case discussion begins.
2
Framing
Defining AI — Without Sounding Academic
~0:05–0:12
Goal: give the room a working definition that's useful, not technical. Then immediately show that "AI" is not one thing.

"For this session, let's define AI as: systems that can recognize patterns, interpret inputs, generate outputs, or take actions in ways that normally required more human judgment."

Not academic. Not a textbook. Useful for this room. Emphasize "normally required human judgment" — that's what makes it consequential for PMOs.

"Not all AI does the same job. Some AI talks. Some AI generates. Some AI coordinates action. And some things we call AI — don't really think at all."

→ This leads directly into the types. Walk them from foundation to frontier.
3
Content Core
The AI Stack — Foundation to Action
~0:12–0:35
Goal: build a shared vocabulary. Walk from ML (the foundation) through to Agentic (the frontier). Use RPA as a contrast to calibrate expectations — not as a main category.
Machine Learning Learns from data — predicts, classifies, detects patterns
This is the foundation. ML models find patterns in historical data and use them to make predictions or decisions. It powers almost everything else — including the models that generate language.
"Think of ML as the engine. Most AI products you use are built on top of ML. It's not magic — it's math applied to large datasets."

"Machine learning is the intellectual foundation of modern AI. It doesn't follow rules someone programmed. It learns patterns from data — and that distinction matters enormously for how you use it."

Generative AI / LLMs Creates content — text, code, summaries, analysis
Large Language Models are ML models trained on massive text corpora using a Transformer architecture — which means they process entire sequences at once instead of word by word. That's why they understand context so well. For PMOs: status reports, risk narratives, executive summaries, minutes, briefings.
"Generating content is useful. But generating content is not the same as creating value."

"LLMs — Large Language Models — are what most people mean when they say 'AI' today. ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, Copilot. They are built on a type of architecture called a Transformer."

"The Transformer does one thing very well: it reads an entire context at once — not word by word — and learns which parts of the input are most relevant to each other. That's why these models understand nuance, tone, and intent. Executively: they are very good at language because they were trained on almost all of the world's written language."

Keep the LLM / Transformer explanation at exactly this level. This audience doesn't need the math — they need the intuition. One sentence on architecture, one on capability, one on relevance to their work. Done.
Conversational AI Interacts — receives questions, interprets intent, responds
Chatbots, internal assistants, intake agents, FAQ support for PMs and stakeholders. Often powered by LLMs but optimized for dialogue flow, not generation.
"Conversational AI reduces friction in access to knowledge. It is less about doing the work — more about improving the interface with the work."

"Think about the experience of having to read a 60-page policy document to find one answer — versus asking a question and getting it in three sentences. That's the value of conversational AI: it changes the interface, not the underlying knowledge."

Agentic AI Coordinates & acts — plans, uses tools, fires tasks, holds memory
Goes beyond answering or generating. An agent can detect a risk signal, consult multiple sources, propose mitigations, draft a stakeholder message, and trigger a task — all toward a defined goal. Can operate as a single agent or as multi-agent systems with human oversight.
"The more autonomy you want, the more you need clarity, governance, and trust."

"Agentic AI is the frontier. It doesn't just answer — it acts. It can plan a sequence of steps, use tools, keep context between actions, and work toward a goal across time."

"For PMOs, this is where it gets interesting and where governance matters most. An agent that can flag risks and notify stakeholders is useful. An agent that can also reschedule tasks and draft remediation plans and notify vendors — that requires real oversight design."

RPA — Robotic Process Automation Follows rules — executes steps, repeats tasks, automates process
Not AI in the strict sense — but useful. Fast, reliable, cost-effective when the process is clear and stable. Doesn't learn or adapt. Just executes.
"RPA is not the same as AI. Sometimes it's exactly the right tool. Not every problem needs a bazooka."

"I mention RPA not because it's AI — it isn't, really — but because it's often confused with AI and because it sometimes is the right answer. If the task is repetitive, rule-based, and stable, RPA will get you there faster, cheaper, and more reliably than most AI approaches."

"The point is this: the mistake many organizations make is trying to solve every problem with the most exciting form of AI, instead of the most appropriate one."

The Quick Version — Say This in the Room
"If I simplify it: Machine Learning learns. Generative AI creates. Conversational AI interacts. Agentic AI coordinates and acts. And RPA just follows instructions — reliably, without drama."
4
Context Shift
AI Then vs. AI Now — Why This Moment Is Different
~0:35–0:45
Goal: validate that people have already lived with AI for years — but show that what changed is qualitative, not just quantitative. Elevate ambition without overselling.

"A few years ago, most people experienced AI as a convenience layer: recommendations, recipes, autocomplete, simple chatbots, pattern recognition in narrow tasks. Useful, but limited."

"What changed is not only model capability. What changed is that now AI can participate in real work: understanding context, generating content, supporting judgment, interacting with tools, and in some cases coordinating action."

"Before, AI was mostly something you consumed. Now it is becoming something you can design into operating models."
This phrase is the emotional arc of the session. It takes people from "AI is a feature in my apps" to "AI is a design decision in how my PMO operates." Land it with confidence — don't rush past it.
5
PMO Lens
From "What Is AI?" to "What Does AI Fix in My PMO?"
~0:45–1:00
Goal: anchor the session to PMO-specific value. Don't lecture on PMO theory — use a filter that forces people to connect AI types to real organizational pain.

"In PMOs, AI does not matter because it is new. It matters if — and only if — it improves one of four things: clarity, foresight, decision quality, or flow of execution."

"So the question is not 'Where can we use AI?' The better question is: 'Where is our PMO losing time, judgment, visibility, or influence today?'"

This is the pivot into the dynamic. Once they have a diagnosis of where pain lives in their own PMO, the 2×2 exercise becomes real — not hypothetical.
"The best AI opportunity is not the most impressive use case. It is the one that sits at the intersection of business pain, process readiness, data availability, and adoption reality."
→ This leads into the Value 2×2 dynamic. Tables now map their own AI ideas onto Value vs. Readiness axes.
6
Dynamics
Value 2×2 → CEO Challenge
~1:00–1:30 + post-break
Goal: move from concepts to positions. The 2×2 surfaces the gap between enthusiasm and readiness. The CEO Challenge forces execution-grade clarity.

"I want each table to place your AI ideas on two axes. The first axis: business value — how meaningfully does this move the needle for the PMO? The second: readiness — do we actually have the process clarity, data, ownership, and sponsorship to execute this now?"

"An AI idea is not strong because it sounds smart. It is strong if the organization can actually adopt it."
Let tables work for 10–12 minutes. As you circulate, push back on ideas in the high-value/high-readiness quadrant: "What makes you confident on readiness? What's the data story here? Who owns this?" You want to surface hidden assumptions, not validate easy answers.

"Now imagine your CFO or CEO says: 'Fine. You have budget for one AI move this year. Why this one?'"

Post-break framing. This single question changes the energy in the room. Before the break, people were enthusiastic. After this question, they need to justify, prioritize, and defend. That tension is the value of the second half.
7
Close
Session Close + Bridge to AI-4
Final ~15 min
Goal: convergence, not summary. Pull 3–5 insights from the room. Close with a filter — not a conclusion. Leave them wanting the roadmap conversation.

"Today is not about leaving with all the answers. It's about leaving with a better filter. Not every AI idea is valuable — and not every valuable idea is ready."

"AI is not the strategy. It is an enabler inside a strategy. The organizations that will move well on this are the ones that start by getting clear on where the real pain lives — not the ones that buy the most tools."

"The biggest risk is not moving too slowly. It's moving noisily — without value logic."
→ Bridge to AI-4: "Once you know the difference between ML, generative, conversational, and agentic AI, the next executive question is inevitable: So what do we do first? That's exactly what Session AI-4 is built to answer."
